SECTION 1: THE SUVAT VARIABLE MATRIX
------------------------------------------------------------------
Before deriving the equations of motion, ensure complete clarity
on the core tracking variables and their SI base units:
  s = Displacement (meters, m)
  u = Initial Velocity (meters per second, m/s)
  v = Final Velocity (meters per second, m/s)
  a = Constant Acceleration (meters per second squared, m/s^2)
  t = Time Elapsed (seconds, s)

------------------------------------------------------------------
SECTION 2: CORE SUVAT DERIVATIONS & MATHEMATICAL PROOFS
------------------------------------------------------------------

CONSTRUCTION OF EQUATION 1: The Velocity-Time Relationship
- By fundamental definition, acceleration is the uniform rate of
  change of velocity over time:
    a = (v - u) / t
- Multiplying both sides by t:
    at = v - u
- Rearranging to isolate final velocity (v):
    v = u + at  [EQUATION 1]

CONSTRUCTION OF EQUATION 2: The Displacement-Velocity Relationship
- For an object undergoing uniform acceleration, the average velocity
  is the arithmetic mean of its initial and final velocities:
    Average Velocity = (u + v) / 2
- Displacement is defined as average velocity multiplied by total time:
    s = [ (u + v) / 2 ] * t  [EQUATION 2]

CONSTRUCTION OF EQUATION 3: Substituting Velocity for Time-Independence
- To eliminate final velocity 'v', substitute Equation 1 (v = u + at)
  directly into Equation 2:
    s = [ (u + (u + at)) / 2 ] * t
    s = [ (2u + at) / 2 ] * t
    s = [ u + (1/2)at ] * t
    s = ut + (1/2)at^2   [EQUATION 3]

CONSTRUCTION OF EQUATION 4: The Time-Independent Formula
- Rearrange Equation 1 to isolate time: t = (v - u) / a
- Substitute this expression for 't' into Equation 2:
    s = [ (v + u) / 2 ] * [ (v - u) / a ]
    s = (v^2 - u^2) / 2a
- Multiplying both sides by 2a and rearranging to isolate v^2:
    v^2 = u^2 + 2as  [EQUATION 4]

SECTION 3: UNIFORM CIRCULAR MOTION & CENTRIPETAL FORCES
------------------------------------------------------------------
Core Concepts:
1. Linear Speed vs. Angular Velocity:
   An object moving in a circle of radius 'r' at constant speed 'v' 
   completes a full rotation (2*pi*r) in time period 'T'.
   - Linear speed: v = (2*pi*r) / T
   - Angular velocity (w) in rad/s: w = 2*pi / T
   - Relationship: v = r * w

2. The Acceleration Paradox:
   Even though the linear speed 'v' is constant, the object is
   constantly accelerating because its *direction of motion* changes 
   at every point along the circular arc. This change in the velocity
   vector requires a net inward acceleration.
   - Centripetal Acceleration formula: ac = v^2 / r = r * w^2

3. Dynamics of Centripetal Force:
   According to Newton's Second Law (F = ma), a centripetal force (Fc) 
   must act toward the center of the circle to maintain this motion.
   - Centripetal Force formula: Fc = (m * v^2) / r = m * r * w^2

SECTION 4: GUIDED WORKED EXAMPLES
------------------------------------------------------------------
PROBLEM 1 (Linear Mechanics):
A vehicle accelerates uniformly from an initial speed of 10 m/s 
to a final speed of 30 m/s over a distance of 100 meters. 
Calculate the constant acceleration.

Solution:
- Given: u = 10 m/s, v = 30 m/s, s = 100 m
- Formula: v^2 = u^2 + 2as
- Substitution:
    (30)^2 = (10)^2 + 2 * a * 100
    900 = 100 + 200a
    800 = 200a -> a = 4.0 m/s^2

PROBLEM 2 (Circular Motion):
A test mass of 0.5 kg is tied to a string of length 2.0 meters 
and whirled in a horizontal circle at a steady speed of 6.0 m/s. 
Find the tension in the string.

Solution:
- Given: m = 0.5 kg, r = 2.0 m, v = 6.0 m/s
- Tension provides the necessary centripetal force: T = (m * v^2) / r
- Substitution:
    T = (0.5 * 6.0^2) / 2.0
    T = (0.5 * 36) / 2.0
    T = 18 / 2.0 -> T = 9.0 Newtons

PART A: MULTIPLE CHOICE QUESTIONS
1. A particle moves along a circle of radius r with constant speed v. 
   What is its acceleration?
   A) v/r, directed tangentially.
   B) v^2/r, directed away from the center.
   C) v^2/r, directed toward the center.
   D) Zero, since speed is constant.

2. A stone dropped from rest down a dry well hits the bottom in 4.0 seconds. 
   How deep is the well? (Assume g = 10 m/s^2)
   A) 40 m    B) 80 m    C) 160 m   D) 200 m

PART B: STRUCTURED THEORY QUESTIONS
3. A stone is projected horizontally from the top of an 80m cliff 
   with an initial horizontal velocity of 15 m/s. Neglecting air resistance:
   (a) Calculate the time taken to hit the ground.
   (b) Determine the horizontal displacement (range) from the cliff base.

4. Distinguish clearly between linear speed and angular velocity, 
   and state why an object in uniform circular motion can never be in 
   transitional equilibrium.

SECTION 6: STEP-BY-STEP MARKING SCHEME & SOLUTIONS KEY
------------------------------------------------------------------
ANSWERS TO PART A:
1. C — Uniform circular motion requires an inward acceleration of v^2/r.
2. B — s = ut + 0.5*g*t^2 -> s = 0 + 0.5 * 10 * (4^2) = 5 * 16 = 80 meters.

ANSWERS TO PART B:
3. (a) Vertical fall: y = 0.5*g*t^2 -> 80 = 5*t^2 -> t^2 = 16 -> t = 4.0 seconds.
   (b) Range: x = vx * t -> x = 15 m/s * 4.0 s = 60 meters.

4. Linear speed is distance covered along the arc per unit time (v = ds/dt).
   Angular velocity is the angle swept at the center per unit time (w = d_theta/dt).
   An object in uniform circular motion experiences a net unbalanced inward force 
   (centripetal force), meaning acceleration is non-zero, violating the condition 
   for static or transitional equilibrium (sum of forces must equal zero).
